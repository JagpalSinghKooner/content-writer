### PAA Questions (8 Documented)

1. **Q:** Will an app actually help my child, or are these just gimmicks designed to sell products?
   **Answer Approach:** Validate scepticism. Cite CHADD research showing apps improve trained tasks but limited real-world transfer. Be honest about limitations while showing where HushAway differs (passive, no training required).

2. **Q:** How do I choose the right app when there are so many options?
   **Answer Approach:** Break down by executive function domain (working memory vs planning vs inhibition). Help parents identify their child's specific struggles first. Position HushAway as the baseline that supports all domains.

3. **Q:** My child is on ADHD medication - why is he still struggling with organisation and planning?
   **Answer Approach:** Explain that medication improves attention but doesn't teach skills (cite research). Position apps and sound support as the skill-building layer that medication enables.

4. **Q:** How much screen time is too much, and won't using an app just add to screen time problems?
   **Answer Approach:** Acknowledge legitimate concern. Distinguish between passive screen time and structured app use. Position HushAway as zero-screen alternative (audio-only option).

5. **Q:** Does my child need a formal diagnosis before I invest in an app?
   **Answer Approach:** Validate parents on waiting lists. Explain that executive function support helps any child struggling with organisation, regardless of diagnosis. No barrier to trying HushAway (free).

6. **Q:** Which specific apps do other UK parents recommend?
   **Answer Approach:** Include real recommendations with parent voice. Tiimo, Brili, ChomChom, Goally. Add HushAway as the foundation layer UK parents are discovering.

7. **Q:** What should I do if my child refuses to use the app, or stops using it after initial interest?
   **Answer Approach:** Normalise this (very common with ADHD). Explain novelty-seeking and loss of interest. Position HushAway as solution - passive, doesn't require engagement or motivation.

8. **Q:** Will using an app help my child develop independence, or will they become dependent on it?
   **Answer Approach:** Address legitimate concern. Explain scaffolding vs crutch distinction. Research shows neurodivergent individuals may need ongoing supports (like glasses for vision). Reframe as accommodation, not failure.

**Option 1: The Executive Function Catch-22**
- Angle: Executive function apps ask children to plan, remember, and organise, the very skills they lack, creating an impossible loop where the tool requires what it's meant to teach.
- Psychology: Names the frustrating paradox parents feel but can't articulate. When you name the impossible situation, parents feel seen and trust you to offer something different.
- Headline direction: "The Cruel Irony of Executive Function Apps (And What Actually Helps)"
- Pattern: NEW - Paradox/catch-22

**Option 2: The Passive Revolution**
- Angle: Stop asking your ADHD child to DO something. The most effective executive function support is the support that requires nothing from them.
- Psychology: Challenges the dominant assumption that apps = active engagement. Flipping this creates intrigue and positions HushAway as genuinely different.
- Headline direction: "Executive Function Apps Keep Failing. Here's Why Doing Less Might Help More."
- Pattern: NEW - Counter-intuitive challenge

**Option 3: The Homework Battleground**
- Angle: That moment when homework is due, your child's brain has shut down, and no amount of apps, timers, or colour-coded planners can cut through the overwhelm, that's where most executive function support fails.
- Psychology: Grounds abstract "executive function" in a specific, visceral moment every parent recognises. Specificity builds instant relatability.
- Headline direction: "When the Homework Meltdown Hits and Every App Fails, What Actually Works?"
- Pattern: NEW - Specificity/scenario

**Option 4: The Apps Without the Nagging**
- Angle: If you're exhausted from reminding your child to use the app that's supposed to stop you reminding them, you're not failing, you've just been given tools that create more work, not less.
- Psychology: Gives permission to stop the cycle. Removes guilt from parents who feel they're "not using the app right."
- Headline direction: "Executive Function Apps Shouldn't Need You to Nag. Here's a Better Way."
- Pattern: NEW - Permission giving
