## Caption

Music isn't working tonight.

.
.
.

Your child's favourite lullaby has failed three times. They're still wide-eyed, wired, nowhere near sleep.

Here's what no one tells you: the problem might not be the song. It might be that their brain can't handle a song right now.

When you're deciding between calming sounds and music for your child, you're not just picking a preference. You're choosing how much you're asking their brain to do.

Music requires processing. Even simple lullabies activate language, memory, motor response, and emotional centres simultaneously. For overwhelmed children, that's too much.

Calming sounds like white noise or pink noise don't ask the brain to do anything. No melody to follow. No lyrics to decode. Just sound.

The framework we use: Ask yourself one question. Is my child's brain available right now?

If yes (regulated, alert) â†’ music can enhance the moment.
If no (tired, overwhelmed) â†’ sounds support without adding load.

Music isn't wrong. Sounds aren't magic. They're just different tools for different moments.

Most parents develop an instinct for this within a few days of paying attention. You don't need to overthink it. You just need both tools in your toolkit.

Because sometimes the most calming thing isn't a song. It's simply sound. Present, steady, asking nothing at all.
