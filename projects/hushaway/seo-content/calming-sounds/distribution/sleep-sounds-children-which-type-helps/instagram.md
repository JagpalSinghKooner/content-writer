You've tried the white noise. The rain sounds. Every "relaxing bedtime sounds" option the app store threw at you. And your child is still wide awake.

Here's what nobody tells you: there's no universal "best" sound. The sound that finally works is the one that matches your child's specific sleep problem.

A racing brain needs different audio than sensory sensitivity. Transition anxiety requires something different again. Once you understand the match, bedtime sounds kids actually respond to become possible to find.

**The four sleep barriers:**

Racing brain: Layered soundscapes with gentle variation. Gives the brain something to follow without demanding attention.

Sensory sensitivity: Carefully calibrated sounds with controlled frequency ranges. No harsh frequencies that trigger sensitive ears.

Transition anxiety: Gentle, continuous audio that bridges the gap. No clear endings. Presence without expectation.

Environmental sensitivity: Masking sounds that cover external noise. Brown or pink noise often better than white noise.

**How to find the match:**

Tonight, watch your child at bedtime. Really watch. Listen to what they say. Notice what happens. Then choose sounds designed for their specific barrier.

Give each sound type at least three consecutive nights before deciding. Your child's nervous system needs time to build the association.
