### Tweet 2
1. RACING BRAIN

The child who can't switch off. Lies in bed replaying the day, worrying about tomorrow.

25-50% of children with ADHD struggle with this nightly.

What works: Layered soundscapes with gentle variation. Gives the brain something to follow without demanding attention.

### Tweet 3
2. SENSORY SENSITIVITY

The child who covers their ears at sounds others barely notice. Can hear the fridge humming two rooms away.

Research shows 80% of autistic children experience sleep disturbances and sensory sensitivities together.

What works: Carefully calibrated sounds with controlled frequency ranges.

### Tweet 4
3. TRANSITION ANXIETY

Bedtime isn't just about sleep. It's about leaving the day behind. Being alone in the dark.

For anxious children, these transitions are genuinely frightening.

What works: Gentle, continuous audio. No clear endings. Low, steady tones that create a sense of being held.

### Tweet 5
4. ENVIRONMENTAL SENSITIVITY

This child can't settle because of what they hear around them. Traffic. Neighbours. The house settling.

Every sound resets the settling process.

What works: Masking sounds. Brown or pink noise often better than white noise. Consistent, enveloping audio.

### Tweet 6
Most parents have been cycling through sounds at random.

White noise. Rain sounds. Ocean waves.

Nothing works.

Here's why: you're trying sounds designed for different problems than the one your child actually has.

### Tweet 7
The shift:

Stop guessing. Start observing.

Watch your child at bedtime tonight. Really watch. Listen to what they say. Notice what happens.

Then choose sounds designed for THAT specific barrier.

### Tweet 8
Testing rule:

Give each sound type at least 3 consecutive nights before deciding.

Your child's nervous system needs time to recognise the sound as safe and associated with sleep.

One night isn't enough data.

### Tweet 9
When sounds don't work alone:

Also consider timing (circadian rhythms vary), environment (light, temperature, textures), routine (predictable sequences), and the day (processing overwhelm from hours ago).

Sounds work best as part of a thoughtful whole.
