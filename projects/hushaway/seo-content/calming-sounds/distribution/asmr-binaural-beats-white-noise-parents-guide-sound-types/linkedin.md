So here's the honest picture:

**ASMR for children:**
Research shows measurable benefits in adults (heart rate reduction, positive emotions). But most studies focus on adults. For children, we have growing anecdotal evidence‚Äîit works brilliantly for some, not at all for others. Safety isn't the concern. Match is.

**Binaural beats:**
Theory: brain entrains to specific frequencies for relaxation or focus. Reality: research is mixed. Some studies show benefits. Others show no significant effect vs placebo. Requires headphones. Avoid if seizure history. Worth trying, but manage expectations.

**White, pink, brown noise:**
All provide masking of environmental disruptions. White is harsh and bright. Pink is gentler (reduced high frequencies). Brown is warm and grounding. Children with sound sensitivity often prefer pink or brown. Strong evidence for masking benefits in adults; less research on children specifically.

The limitations matter:
We don't have large-scale studies on long-term effects for children. We don't have neurodivergent-specific research. We don't have direct comparisons between sound types.

But that doesn't mean these sounds don't help.

It means you try them with appropriate expectations. You observe your child's actual response. You make decisions based on what works for your specific family.

Unpopular opinion:

Telling parents that binaural beats "definitely work" for children is marketing, not honesty.

Here's what the research actually shows:

A 2017 systematic review in Frontiers in Psychiatry examined multiple studies on binaural beat efficacy.

The findings? Mixed.

Some studies showed benefits for anxiety reduction and focus. Others showed no significant effect compared to placebo or regular music.

The researchers' conclusion: "While some positive effects exist, evidence is inconsistent and study quality varies considerably."

So why do some families swear by binaural beats while others see zero change?

Because individual response varies. Massively.
