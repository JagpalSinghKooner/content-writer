## Text Post 1

The rain sounds worked perfectly for bedtime. So you tried them for homework. Complete disaster.

Here's what nobody tells you about finding sounds for an anxious child:

The same sound that works magic at 8pm can make 4pm homework time worse. The sound that settles them in the car might be completely wrong for getting out the door in the morning.

Your child's nervous system isn't in the same state all day. An anxious brain needs something different than a distracted brain. A brain overwhelmed by a transition needs something different again.

So here's how to match the right sound to the right moment:

**For anxiety (racing thoughts, physical tension):**
Simple, repetitive sounds. Gentle rain. Brown noise. Low frequency tones. Give the anxious brain something steady to latch onto without adding to the processing load.

**For focus (homework, learning tasks):**
White or pink noise. Binaural beats in the beta range. Very simple ambient music without lyrics. ADHD brains crave stimulation. The right sound satisfies that need without becoming a distraction.

**For transitions (morning routines, after-school reset, activity changes):**
Gentle sounds that build slowly. Consistent sound cues that signal "change is coming." Have calming sounds already playing when they walk through the door. Predictability helps the brain prepare.

**For car journeys:**
Short trips: Brown noise or nature sounds. Long trips: Gentle storytelling or layered ambient soundscapes. Masks unpredictable road noise with something consistent.

The families who make sound work build their toolkit in advance. When things are calm. When there's space to experiment.

## Text Post 2

"My tummy hurts."

Sound familiar?

When anxiety sits in the body, your child might not even recognise they're anxious. They just feel unwell. Tense. Something's wrong but they can't name it.

This is physical anxiety. And it needs a different approach than mental anxiety (racing thoughts, catastrophising).

Here's what works:

**Low frequency sounds.**

Brown noise. Binaural beats in the theta range (4-7 Hz). Soft humming tones.

Why? Lower frequencies can help activate the parasympathetic nervous system. That's the "rest and digest" response. The opposite of the fight-or-flight state creating those physical symptoms.

When to play these sounds:
• Tummy aches before school
• When they seem physically wound up but can't explain why
• During recovery after a big emotional moment

The key is matching the sound to what's actually happening in their nervous system. Not just playing "calming sounds" and hoping for the best.

Your child's body is trying to tell them something. The right sound can help them hear it.

## Text Post 3

You know what surprises most parents?

For many children with ADHD, silence is actually the worst environment for focus.

Their brain goes hunting for stimulation and finds it everywhere except the homework in front of them. The bird outside. The itch on their arm. The memory of something that happened at lunch. Everything is more interesting than the worksheet.

The right sound can provide that stimulation in a way that helps focus rather than distracts from it.

Here's what works for homework and learning tasks:

**White or pink noise. Binaural beats in the beta range (13-30 Hz). Very simple ambient music without lyrics.**

Research shows that background sound can help the brain tune out distractions and maintain focus. For ADHD brains, the stimulation from white noise can actually improve task performance by satisfying the brain's need for input.

But here's what matters most: **consistency.**

Play the same sound from the start of homework time every session. Let the sound become a cue that signals "focus time." Playing it some days but not others makes it less effective.

And what to avoid: Music with lyrics. Sounds that change frequently. Anything your child actively listens to rather than having as background.

The goal isn't to distract from distraction. It's to give the brain the input it needs so it can settle into the task.
