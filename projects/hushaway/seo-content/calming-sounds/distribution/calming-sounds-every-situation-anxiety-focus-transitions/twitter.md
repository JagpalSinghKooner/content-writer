### Tweet 2
Your child's nervous system isn't in the same state all day.

An anxious brain needs something different than a distracted brain.

A brain overwhelmed by a transition needs something different again.

### Tweet 3
1. FOR ANXIETY (racing thoughts, physical tension)

Try: Simple repetitive sounds. Brown noise. Gentle rain.

Why it works: Gives the brain a steady anchor without adding to the processing load.

Play it early. Don't wait until anxiety peaks.

### Tweet 4
2. FOR FOCUS (homework, learning tasks)

Try: White or pink noise. Binaural beats (beta range 13-30 Hz).

Why it works: ADHD brains crave stimulation. White noise satisfies that need without becoming a distraction.

Key: Use the SAME sound every session.

### Tweet 5
3. FOR TRANSITIONS (morning routines, after-school reset)

Try: Gentle sounds that build slowly. Consistent cues 5 minutes before change.

Why it works: Predictability helps the brain prepare for change.

Have sound already playing when they walk in the door.

### Tweet 6
4. FOR CAR JOURNEYS

Short trips (under 30 min): Brown noise or nature sounds
Long trips (30+ min): Gentle storytelling, ambient soundscapes

Why it works: Masks unpredictable road noise with something consistent.

### Tweet 7
5. FOR CREATIVE TIME (art, building, play)

Try: Nature sounds without harsh elements. Very simple ambient soundscapes.

Why it works: Creative tasks need room for the mind to wander. Too much stimulation blocks that flow.

### Tweet 8
Here's the mistake most parents make:

They wait until a child is mid-meltdown to introduce a new sound.

The families who make sound work build their toolkit in advance. When things are calm. When there's space to experiment.

### Tweet 9
Quick action steps:

1. Write down your 4-5 trickiest moments
2. Match sounds using the guide above
3. Test during calm moments first
4. Create easy access (saved playlists, speaker ready)
5. Introduce early, not during crisis

## Single Tweets

### Tweet A (Insight)
"My tummy hurts."

When anxiety sits in the body, your child might not recognise they're anxious. They just feel unwell.

Try: Low frequency sounds. Brown noise. Binaural beats in theta range (4-7 Hz).

Lower frequencies activate the parasympathetic nervous system. Rest over fight-or-flight.

### Tweet B (Hot Take)
Unpopular opinion:

For ADHD children, silence is actually the WORST environment for focus.

Their brain goes hunting for stimulation and finds it everywhere except the homework.

White noise satisfies that craving without becoming a distraction.

### Tweet C (Practical Tip)
Here's the transition hack that changes mornings:

Start calming sounds 15-20 minutes BEFORE you need them up.

Let the sound gently wake them rather than adding sound after they're already awake and struggling.

This simple shift can transform your mornings.

### Tweet D (Problem/Solution)
The car journey to Grandma's shouldn't feel like defusing a bomb.

But car journeys combine several challenges: confinement, unpredictable duration, sensory input, limited entertainment.

The fix: Consistent background sound that masks unpredictable road noise.

Ready before you get in.

### Tweet E (Research-Backed)
Research shows background sound can help the brain tune out distractions and maintain focus.

For ADHD brains specifically, white noise can improve task performance by satisfying the brain's need for input.

But only if you use the SAME sound consistently. Let it become a "focus time" cue.
