## Text Post 1

Your child isn't broken because Headspace doesn't work for them.

Here's what no one tells you: every mainstream calming app on the market was designed for neurotypical children first. The ND-friendly versions? Adaptations. Marketing. Not redesigns.

The difference matters.

When your autistic child can't follow guided meditation, it's not because they're "not trying hard enough." It's because their brain processes auditory instructions differently. Metaphors like "imagine your worries as leaves on a stream" create confusion, not calm.

When your ADHD child can't sit through breath exercises, it's not a personal failing. ADHD brains have different dopamine regulation. They need something to land on. Silence isn't calming—it's an invitation for racing thoughts to get louder.

Here's what actually works:

**Passive listening.** Sound that requires nothing from your child. No following instructions. No making choices. No interactive elements. Just press play.

This isn't lazy design. It's smart design. When a child is overwhelmed, every demand you remove helps their nervous system settle faster.

**Sensory-safe design.** No sudden volume changes. No unexpected sound effects. Predictable, consistent audio the nervous system can trust.

**Zero-demand principle.** The child's only job is to exist in the presence of the sound. They don't have to breathe a certain way, visualise anything, or follow along.

The apps you've been trying weren't designed for your child. They were designed for easier children and marketed to yours.

When calming apps don't work, the design failed. Not your child. Not you.

## Text Post 2

After 7 years running a nursery, I've watched hundreds of parents blame themselves when calming apps don't work for their neurodivergent children.

"We're just not consistent enough."
"Maybe they need more time."
"Every other parent says it works..."

Here's permission to stop:

The failure isn't yours. The apps were designed for different brains.

Most mainstream calming apps share a fundamental assumption: that a child can engage with guided instructions during moments of stress.

For neurotypical children, this works reasonably well. Their prefrontal cortex stays online during moderate stress. They can follow multi-step instructions. They can shift attention from their feelings to an external voice.

But neurodivergent children experience stress differently. During overwhelm—whether that's a meltdown, bedtime anxiety, or sensory flooding—their ability to follow instructions often goes offline entirely.

The thinking part of their brain has already checked out. And no amount of "just breathe" will bring it back.

Research on executive function differences shows that ADHD children struggle with working memory, flexible thinking, and self-control, particularly during stressful moments. The exact moments when calming apps are supposed to help are the moments when ND children can't use them.

This is a design problem, not a child problem.

When you're ready to try something different, look for approaches built from the ground up for neurodivergent children. Not adapted mainstream apps with "ND-friendly" marketing.

Your child isn't failing the app. The app is failing your child.
