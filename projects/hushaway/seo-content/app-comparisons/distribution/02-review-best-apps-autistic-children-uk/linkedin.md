## Text Post 1

It's 11pm. Your autistic child is still awake, overwhelmed by a day that asked too much of their nervous system.

And somewhere on your phone, there's supposed to be an app that helps.

You've downloaded three already this week.

One had a cheerful voice that made your child cover their ears.

Another asked them to "breathe in for four counts" while they were mid-meltdown.

The third had so many menu options that choosing one became its own source of stress.

Here's what most parents don't realise:

Most "autism-friendly" apps weren't designed for autistic processing. They were designed for neurotypical children and marketed to autism families later.

The difference matters.

Research shows up to 90% of autistic individuals experience sensory sensitivities. A voice that seems gentle to neurotypical ears might feel intrusive. Background music meant to relax can become an unpredictable assault.

What autistic children actually need:

• Predictability (no surprises, no novelty for novelty's sake)
• Passive listening (no instructions to follow during overwhelm)
• Sensory-safe audio (consistent volume, smooth transitions)
• UK voices (familiar accents reduce cognitive load)

Moshi works for some families, but requires parent curation. Calm and Headspace rely on guided meditation that fights against autistic processing. Smiling Mind is free but Australian-accented and technically unreliable.

The apps designed specifically for autism from the ground up? Those are the ones that actually work.

Because when an app respects how autistic brains work instead of fighting against them, sound becomes genuinely calming.

Have you found an app that works for your child? What made the difference?

## Text Post 2

"Why don't meditation apps work for my autistic child?"

I hear this question constantly.

The answer is simple but important:

Meditation apps assume children can follow instructions, maintain attention through voice guidance, and engage with visualisation exercises.

During moments of stress or sensory overload, many autistic children cannot provide these things.

The app asks them to:
• Close your eyes
• Follow this breath pattern
• Imagine you're floating on a cloud
• Listen and do what I say

But when a child's system is already overwhelmed, these aren't requests. They're additional demands on an overloaded nervous system.

Calm, Headspace, Smiling Mind — all built around guided meditation. All requiring active participation. All fighting against autistic processing rather than working with it.

The apps that actually help autistic children during hard moments?

They ask nothing.

Press play. That's it.

No choices to make. No instructions to follow. No visualisation required.

The sound does the work while your child does nothing except exist in its presence.

This isn't lazy design. It's respectful design.

Because the best support during overwhelm isn't another demand. It's predictable, sensory-safe sound that the autistic nervous system can trust completely.

What's your experience? Do guided apps work for your child, or do they add to the stress?

## Text Post 3

Quick comparison for autism families looking at calming apps:

Apps ADAPTED for autism:
• Start with neurotypical design
• Add "autism-friendly" label later
• Require engagement and choices
• Use US voices and references
• Include unpredictable audio elements

Apps DESIGNED for autism:
• Start with autistic processing
• Build sensory-safe audio from the ground up
• Require nothing from your child
• Use UK voices and familiar context
• Eliminate sensory triggers completely

The first group includes Moshi, Calm, Headspace. They work for some autistic children some of the time, but only after parents curate content and find what doesn't trigger.

The second group is much smaller. But when an app is designed for how autistic children actually process sound, it works differently.

No trial and error. No previewing content at midnight. No wondering if this track will have a sudden sound effect that startles.

Just press play.

If you're exhausted from downloading apps that don't work, this distinction is why.

Not all "autism-friendly" means autism-designed.
