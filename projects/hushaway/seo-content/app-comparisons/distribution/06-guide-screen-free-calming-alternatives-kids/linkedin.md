## Text Post 2

"Is Zenimal worth it for my autistic child?"

I see this question constantly in parent groups.

Here's what nobody tells you: Zenimal solves the screen problem perfectly. But it creates a content problem.

9 meditations. 3 sleep soundscapes. That's it.

For neurotypical children who respond well to guided meditation, it works reasonably well. But ND children often need very specific sounds for very specific situations.

A child who responds beautifully to nature soundscapes might completely reject guided voice content. A child who needs binaural frequencies won't find them on Zenimal. No ASMR. No frequency-based sounds. No solfeggio tones.

The National Autistic Society's guidance emphasises that autistic children experience sound in highly individual ways. What soothes one child may distress another.

With only 12 total audio options, there's little room to find alternatives if the available options don't suit your child's sensory profile.

At £63-65, it's a significant investment for potentially unsuitable content.

This is why I talk about the hybrid approach. An app on a Bluetooth speaker gives you screen-free delivery without locking you into a fixed content library. Room to explore what actually works for your specific child.

Zenimal isn't wrong. It's just very limited for the way neurodivergent children actually experience sound.

## Text Post 3

Physical sensory tools don't replace sound-based calming. They complement it.

This distinction matters more than you'd think.

I see parents in "one or the other" thinking. Calming toys vs apps. Screen-free vs sound library. Physical tools vs audio content.

But here's what happens in real life:

**3pm after-school meltdown:** Your child is auditory overloaded. They need silence or very specific soothing sound. They can't engage with a fidget cube. Sound-based calming is what works in this moment.

**Homework time:** They need focus support. Binaural beats through headphones help their ADHD brain concentrate. Physical tools can't deliver brainwave entrainment.

**Bedtime anxiety:** Racing thoughts. Can't switch off. A story or frequency sound helps. A weighted blanket provides comfort, but doesn't address the thought loop.

**Mid-meltdown:** They're beyond words, beyond engaging with anything. They need proprioceptive input. The weighted lap pad or body sock is what brings them back. Sound won't reach them right now.

Different regulation needs require different tools.

Every family raising a neurodivergent child should have sensory tools available. Weighted blankets, fidgets, chewelry, crash pads, kinetic sand—these are essential.

But they address physical sensory channels. They don't replace auditory regulation, sleep onset support, or nervous system calming through frequency.

The question isn't calming toys vs apps. It's which tool works for which situation.

Your toolkit needs both. Not one instead of the other. Both.

What's in your regulation toolkit? What works for which moments?
