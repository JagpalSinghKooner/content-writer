## Text Post 2

Between 50-80% of neurodivergent children struggle with sleep.

Compare that to 20-30% of neurotypical children.

This isn't a discipline problem. This is neurology.

The ADHD brain won't switch off. During the day, there are distractions. At night, when everything goes quiet, every thought from breakfast comes rushing back.

The autistic brain craves predictability. Bedtime involves at least 7 transitions in under an hour. Each transition is a moment where something could be different.

Sensory systems don't clock off. The scratch of cotton sheets. The distant TV. The smell of washing powder. For children with sensory processing differences, these aren't background details. They're active inputs.

Melatonin timing differs. Research shows neurodivergent children often have delayed melatonin production. Their bodies signal "time to sleep" later than their peers.

Anxiety peaks in the dark. Daytime provides distractions. At night, in a quiet dark room, anxious thoughts have nothing to compete with.

Understanding this changes everything.

You can stop applying neurotypical strategies to a neurodivergent brain. You can stop blaming yourself. You can start using approaches that actually work.

Sound isn't optional for neurodivergent bedtime routines. It's the mechanism that makes everything else work.

Why? Because sound requires nothing from your child. No interaction. No choices. No demands. It works on the nervous system without asking anything in return.

Pink noise for sleep onset. ASMR textures for anxious children. Layered ambient sounds for ADHD racing thoughts. Consistent, predictable sounds for autistic brains seeking sameness.

## Text Post 3

The visual schedule shows "story time."

The soft ambient sounds confirm the phase.

Your child's brain receives two consistent signals. They see what's happening. They hear what's happening.

This is how visual and auditory supports work better together than either alone.

Most resources treat them separately. Visual schedules in one section. Sound mentioned briefly in another.

But layering them creates something more powerful:

The visual shows structure. The sound provides regulation.

The visual makes the routine predictable. The sound gives the nervous system what it needs to actually settle.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

Bath picture + gentle upbeat sounds
→ Pyjamas picture + same sounds continuing
→ Teeth picture + sounds continuing
→ Story picture + sounds lower or off
→ Bed picture + sleep sounds begin

Each picture matches a sound phase. Consistency from multiple senses.

For neurodivergent children who struggle with:
• Transitions (sound bridges the gaps)
• Uncertainty (visual + sound = double confirmation)
• Processing demands (passive listening requires nothing)
• Separation anxiety (sound stays when parent leaves)

This isn't about adding more complexity. It's about making the routine work through multiple channels.

Your 4-week implementation:

Week 1: Nail down the structure (consistent sequence every night)
Week 2: Add visual supports (make structure visible)
Week 3: Layer in sound (start at lights-off, find what works)
Week 4+: Extend sound through earlier phases if helpful

Give each phase at least 3 nights before judging. Neurodivergent brains need time to accept new patterns.

What to expect:

Some children respond immediately. Others need 2-3 weeks. Don't judge after one night.

Some nights will work better than others. That's normal. Consistency matters more than perfection.

You'll learn what your child's brain needs. More complex sounds? Softer textures? Different volumes? The first week is discovery.
