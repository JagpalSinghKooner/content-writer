## Caption

Your ADHD child won't go to bed. You've tried everything. Earlier bedtimes. Screen-free evenings. Calm environments. Nothing sticks.

.
.
.

Here's what the advice misses: an ADHD brain at bedtime isn't being defiant. It's doing exactly what ADHD brains do. Racing. Seeking. Filling silence because silence feels unbearable.

Picture your child's brain like a radio scanning for a station. There's nothing to tune into. So it creates its own noise. Worries. Questions. Stories. Anything to fill the void.

Generic "wind-down time" doesn't work because there's nothing for the brain to land on. The silence you're creating isn't calm for them. It's a vacuum their thoughts rush to fill.

Building an ADHD bedtime routine that actually works means understanding this: that racing brain needs something to land on. Sound gives it an anchor.

Not music with melodies their brain will try to follow. Not podcasts with stories. Passive sound. Ambient. Consistent. Predictable. Sound that fills the silence without demanding anything.

According to the Sleep Foundation, up to 75% of children with ADHD report sleep problems. Three in four. If bedtime is a battle in your house, you're not alone. And you're not failing.

An effective ADHD bedtime routine isn't about stricter rules or more discipline. You don't need to be more consistent. You need to give your child's brain what it actually needs at each stage.

The honest timeline? Expect four to six weeks of consistent practice. ADHD brains need more repetition to build automatic associations. That's the neurology, not your parenting.

But here's what happens when you stick with it: Week 2 brings small improvements. Week 4, the routine starts feeling familiar. Week 6, automatic settling becomes possible. Not every night. But regularly.

One quiet moment can change a whole day for a child. For ADHD children, that quiet moment starts when their racing brain finally has somewhere to settle.
