# Positioning: Bedtime Routines for Neurodivergent Children

## Market Sophistication Analysis

**Stage: 3 (The Mechanism Introducer)**

Parents searching for ND bedtime help have tried:
- Visual schedules (common recommendation, works partially)
- Weighted blankets (helpful but not enough for transition)
- "Calming music" (generic advice everywhere, but WHAT music?)
- Medication (addresses symptoms, not routine)
- Sleep consultants (expensive, often neurotypical-focused)

**Why Stage 3:** Parents are skeptical because generic "try calming sounds" advice hasn't worked. They've heard the promise. Now they need the mechanism — WHY ND-designed sound is different from YouTube playlists, and WHAT specifically to play WHEN.

**The winning approach at Stage 3:** Introduce the mechanism. Not just "sound helps bedtime" but "here's WHY passive, ND-designed sound works when generic calming music doesn't."

## Consistency Check

**All angles share:** Sound as a primary regulation tool that requires nothing from a struggling child. The WHAT gap that no competitor fills. ND-first understanding that different brains need different approaches.

**Primary angle establishes:** Sound is THE core element, not an afterthought. We tell you WHAT to play, WHEN, and WHY.

**Supporting angles extend via:**
- **Article 01 (Foundation):** Validates neurological basis → creates demand for neurological solutions → sets up sound
- **Article 02 (Conversion Driver):** Delivers on primary angle promise → WHAT to play, WHEN, WHY → direct product bridge
- **Article 03 (Visual Integration):** Sound enhances visual schedules → not competition, integration → broader appeal
- **Article 04 (ADHD-Specific):** Racing brain mechanism → sound as anchor → WHY it works for ADHD
- **Article 05 (Autism-Specific):** Predictability mechanism → sound as consistent signal → WHY it works for autism
- **Article 06 (Anxiety):** Presence mechanism → sound stays → WHY it works for separation anxiety

**Potential conflicts:**
- **ADHD vs Autism angles could feel contradictory** (racing brain vs predictability need). Resolution: Both articles acknowledge both needs. ADHD article mentions predictability. Autism article mentions calm-down. They're complementary, not competing.
- **Visual routine article could feel like sound is secondary.** Resolution: Frame as "visuals + sound > visuals alone" rather than "add sound to your visual routine."

**Reader journey:** Someone landing on Article 01 learns bedtime struggles are neurological, then finds Article 02 with specific sound guidance, then explores condition-specific articles (04, 05) or addresses anxiety (06). All roads lead to pillar guide for comprehensive framework. At every point, sound is positioned as primary tool with specific guidance — not generic "try calming music."
