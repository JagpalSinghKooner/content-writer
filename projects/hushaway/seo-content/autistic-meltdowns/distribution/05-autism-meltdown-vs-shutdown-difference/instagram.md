## Caption

The crisis nobody talks about üëá

Your child comes home from school and goes straight to their room without a word. You call after them. Nothing. You knock. Still nothing.

Tired, you think. Or sulking.

Here's what you might be missing: this isn't rest. This is a shutdown.

When we talk about autism meltdowns, most people picture the dramatic version‚Äîthe screaming, the kicking, the visible distress that makes strangers stare.

But there's another response to overwhelm that looks completely different. It's quiet. It's still. And because it doesn't demand attention the way a meltdown does, it often goes completely unrecognised.

Think of it as two different alarm systems. A meltdown is the brain hitting the alarm switch‚Äîsirens blaring, everyone can hear it. A shutdown is the brain hitting the off switch‚Äîcomplete power cut, nobody notices.

Both come from nervous system overload. The difference? How it comes out.

**Meltdown = outward explosion.** Energy bursts out. Crying, screaming, hitting. Loud. Visible. Impossible to ignore.

**Shutdown = inward collapse.** Energy disappears. Withdrawal, silence, stillness, blank stares. Quiet. Invisible. Easy to miss entirely.

A simple way to remember: meltdown is fight-or-flight. Shutdown is freeze.

The child who goes quiet and still after school isn't resting. They're overwhelmed beyond words. Their nervous system has gone offline to protect itself.

And because they're not making noise, nobody notices.

That's the quiet crisis. The one nobody talks about.

If you recognise your child in this, now you have a name for it. Shutdown. And more importantly, tools to help.

**How to help during a shutdown:**
‚Üí Reduce demands to zero (no questions, no requests)
‚Üí Create safety (dim lights, reduce noise)
‚Üí Be present but not intrusive
‚Üí Wait (shutdowns run their course, just like meltdowns)

Sometimes gentle sound helps. Not to add stimulation, but to signal safety. Quiet soundscapes or low frequencies give the nervous system something safe to land on without adding demands.

The child who goes silent after school needs just as much support as the child having visible meltdowns. Maybe more. Because nobody's noticing.

One quiet moment can change a whole day for a child. Sometimes that quiet moment is giving them permission to be quiet themselves.
