## Text Post 1

You're searching your phone at 2am.

Your autistic child is screaming. Or sobbing. Or rigid and unreachable.

"Have you tried calming music?" everyone says.

But what music? Which track? At what volume?

Here's what actually works:

**Brown noise or pink noise.** Deep, consistent, predictable. Think heavy rain on a roof or a distant waterfall. No surprises. No sudden changes. Just a consistent sound blanket that masks unpredictable environmental noise.

**Low-frequency sounds.** Below 500Hz. Physically grounding. Some parents swear by Solfeggio frequencies (396Hz, 528Hz). What matters most is consistency and lack of surprise.

**ASMR sounds for some children.** Gentle tapping, soft brushing, quiet rustling. Not everyone responds, but when it works, it's powerful.

**What to avoid:** Music with lyrics (adds cognitive load). High-pitched sounds (can feel painful). Unpredictable changes (more for the brain to process). Complete silence (lets every other sound through).

Why sound works when other tools fail:

Weighted blankets need your child to tolerate touch. Breathing exercises need cognitive processing. Fidgets need motor control. All unavailable mid-meltdown.

Sound bypasses all of this. No demands. Your child doesn't need to hold anything. Do anything. Engage with anything. They just need to be within hearing distance.

The critical piece most parents miss: test sounds when your child is calm, not during crisis. Play them during quiet time, wind-down, before sleep. The sounds that help when they're regulated are likely to help during overwhelm.

But you need to discover what those are before you need them.

Sound isn't magic. But for many autistic children, it's the tool that works when everything else fails.

## Text Post 2

I've spoken to hundreds of parents of autistic children.

The ones who keep sound as their go-to tool all say the same thing:

"I gave up on it too quickly the first time."

They tried one "calming music" track from YouTube. It didn't work. They wrote off sound entirely.

Here's what they wish they'd known:

**Not all sounds are equal.** Random "calming music" playlists often have lyrics (cognitive load), unpredictable changes (more processing), or high-pitched elements (can feel painful during overwhelm).

What works: Brown noise. Pink noise. Low-frequency sounds. Consistent, predictable, no surprises.

**Volume matters.** Don't blast sound into a crisis. Start quieter than you think. Increase gradually only if it seems to help. Sudden loud sound adds sensory inputâ€”exactly what you don't want.

**It needs time.** Sound won't stop a meltdown instantly. Nothing does. The goal isn't to end the meltdown immediately. It's to give the nervous system something consistent to anchor to while the overwhelm runs its course.

**The critical test happens when calm.** Introduce different sound types during quiet time, wind-down, or before sleep. Notice what your child seems to respond to. Ask them if they're verbal: "Does this sound feel nice or not nice?"

The sounds that help when they're regulated are likely to help during overwhelm. But you need to discover what those are before you need them.

Many parents find sound becomes their most reliable tool once they find what works for their specific child.
