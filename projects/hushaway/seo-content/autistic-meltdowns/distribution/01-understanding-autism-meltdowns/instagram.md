## Caption

That meltdown isn't bad behaviour. It's neurological overflow.

.
.
.

You've been there. Your child is on the floor of the supermarket. Screaming. Maybe kicking. And that stranger is staring at you like you're the world's worst parent.

Here's what nobody tells you: A meltdown is what happens when your child's brain gets more input than it can process. Think computer crash, not defiance.

Research shows up to 90% of autistic individuals process sensory information differently. The sounds you barely notice might be painfully loud. The seams in their socks might feel like sandpaper all day. Fluorescent lights might flicker in a way only they perceive.

Imagine your child has an invisible bucket that fills throughout the day. Every sensory input, every social demand, every unexpected change adds water. When the bucket overflows, you get a meltdown.

The thing that appears to "cause" it (you asked them to brush teeth, took a different route home) is rarely the real cause. It's just the drop that made the bucket overflow.

This is why after-school meltdowns are so common. Your child held it together all day, their bucket filling and filling. Then they get homeâ€”their safe spaceâ€”and it all comes out.

Understanding the difference between a meltdown and a tantrum changes everything. A tantrum is goal-directed (your child wants something). A meltdown has no goal (your child has lost the ability to regulate).

During a meltdown, trying to reason with your child is like trying to have a conversation with someone who's drowning. The thinking part of their brain is offline. Your job isn't to make it stop faster. It's to keep them safe until their nervous system can reset.

- High contrast: Dark text on light background
- Minimal elements per slide (reduce visual clutter)

**Reel B-roll suggestions:**
- Parent sitting beside child (not hovering)
- Quiet, low-stimulus environment
- Visual representation of "bucket filling" (drops of water animation)
- Close-up of sound being played on phone/speaker
- Parent and child in calm moment after recovery

**Key visual hook for Reel:**
- Start with relatable moment: Parent looking exhausted in public space
- Quick cuts to show sensory overload from child's perspective (fast-paced, overwhelming)
- Transition to calm, explaining what's actually happening
- End with hope: parent and child in peaceful moment
