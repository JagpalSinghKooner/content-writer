# Newsletter Snippet: Preventing Autism Meltdowns

## Hook

It wasn't the socks. You know exactly what I mean. Your child had a complete meltdown this morning. Over socks. The seams were wrong. Or the colour was wrong. Or they were the right socks but somehow also wrong. And somewhere in the chaos, you wondered: how is this the thing that broke them?

## Body

Here's what nobody tells you about preventing autism meltdowns. That sock moment wasn't the cause. It was the tenth thing that day.

The alarm that was too loud. The breakfast that touched on the plate. The sibling who breathed too close. The tag in the shirt. The change in routine you forgot to mention. Drop after drop after drop. Then the socks.

Think of your child's nervous system as a bucket. Every sensory input, every social demand, every small frustration adds water to that bucket. A typical school morning might add the scratchy jumper, the assembly hall echo, the child who sat too close, the substitute teacher who changed the routine, the playground noise. Then they come home. And you ask them to put on different socks.

The sock didn't fill the bucket. The sock was just the drop that made it overflow. This is why meltdowns can seem so unpredictable. "But they've worn those socks a hundred times!" Yes. But those hundred other times, the bucket wasn't already at the brim.

Understanding this changes everything. You can't eliminate every trigger. You'd drive yourself mad trying. But you can keep the bucket from overflowing before the socks even come out of the drawer. The shift: stop trying to eliminate triggers. Start building regulation throughout the day. Five minutes here. Ten minutes there. Small moments of calm, of sensory reset, of nervous system support. The bucket empties a little at each opportunity.

Most parents discover sound during or after meltdowns. But sound works better as prevention than as crisis intervention. Your child doesn't need to recognise they're stressed for sound to help regulate stress. No interaction. No decisions. No effort. Just press play. Morning transition. After-school reset. Homework background. Evening wind-down. Throughout the day as needed. The bucket empties a bit at every opportunity.
