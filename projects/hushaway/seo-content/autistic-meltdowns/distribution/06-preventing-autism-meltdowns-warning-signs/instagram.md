## Caption

It wasn't the socks.

You know exactly what I mean. Your child had a complete meltdown this morning. Over socks. The seams were wrong. Or the colour was wrong. Or they were the right socks but somehow also wrong.

And somewhere in the chaos, you wondered: how is this the thing that broke them?

.
.
.

Here's what nobody tells you about preventing autism meltdowns. That sock moment wasn't the cause. It was the tenth thing that day.

The alarm that was too loud. The breakfast that touched on the plate. The sibling who breathed too close. The tag in the shirt. The change in routine you forgot to mention. Drop after drop after drop. Then the socks.

Think of your child's nervous system as a bucket. Every sensory input, every social demand, every small frustration adds water to that bucket. The sock didn't fill the bucket. The sock was just the drop that made it overflow.

.
.
.

This is why meltdowns can seem so unpredictable. "But they've worn those socks a hundred times!" Yes. But those hundred other times, the bucket wasn't already at the brim.

Understanding this changes everything. You can't eliminate every trigger. You'd drive yourself mad trying. But you can keep the bucket from overflowing before the socks even come out of the drawer.

.
.
.

The shift that changes everything: stop trying to eliminate triggers. Start building regulation throughout the day.

Morning transition. After-school reset. Homework background. Evening wind-down. Small moments of calm, of sensory reset, of nervous system support. Five minutes here. Ten minutes there. The bucket empties a little at each opportunity.

Most parents discover sound during or after meltdowns. But sound works better as prevention than as crisis intervention. Your child doesn't need to recognise they're stressed for sound to help regulate stress. No interaction. No decisions. No effort. Just press play.

.
.
.

Before every meltdown, there's a warning period. The "rumbling stage." The bucket is nearly full but hasn't overflowed yet. Learning to spot it is your biggest opportunity for preventing autism meltdowns.

Physical signs: increased fidgeting, covering ears more, faster breathing, tensing up, flushing or pale. Behavioural shifts: rigid about small things, saying "no" to everything, withdrawal, taking longer to respond. Verbal cues: repeating phrases, voice changing pitch, saying "I can't" repeatedly.

Your child might move through these stages in minutes. Or slowly over hours. What matters is learning your child's specific pattern. Because once you can spot the rumble, you can intervene before the meltdown.

.
.
.

The goal isn't a meltdown-free life. The goal is a nervous system that can handle more. A bucket that empties faster than it fills. A baseline of regulation that gives your child capacity for the inevitable challenges.

It was never about the socks.

## Visual Notes

**Carousel Design:**
- Colour palette: Soft, calming colours (dusty blue, sage green, cream)
- Typography: Large, bold headlines with breathing room
- Slide 1: Dramatic text treatment, minimal design
- Slides 2-11: One concept per slide, avoid clutter
- Bucket visual on Slides 4-6: Simple illustration or icon
- Warning signs slides: Bullet points with checkmarks or dots
- Final slide: Warm, reassuring tone

**Reel B-roll Suggestions:**
- Opening: Socks in hand (close-up, natural lighting)
- Bucket visual: Could be animated graphic or simple physical bucket filling with water
- Overlay text: Clean sans-serif, high contrast for readability
- Colour grading: Warm, soft (not harsh fluorescents)
- Music: Gentle, ambient (no jarring sounds)
- Pacing: Calm throughout, not frantic
- Ending: Either face-to-camera for connection OR text overlay with warm background

**Alternative Reel Concept (Parent POV):**
- Film from parent perspective during morning routine
- Overlay text showing each "drop" as it happens
- Visual bucket graphic filling throughout
- Final shot: The socks (and bucket overflowing)
- Voiceover or text: "It was never about the socks"
