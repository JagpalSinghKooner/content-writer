## Caption

Every guide says "try calming music" during autism meltdowns.

What music? Which sounds? At what volume? When they're kicking and thrashing, do you just hold up your phone and hope for the best?

Nobody answers.

.
.
.

Here's what I wish someone had told me when we were drowning.

Meltdowns aren't single events. They have three phases: Prevention (before), During (the crisis), and Recovery (after). Most advice focuses entirely on "during" because it's the dramatic part. But prevention can reduce how often meltdowns happen. And recovery, done poorly, can trigger another meltdown within hours.

The socks didn't cause the meltdown. They were the tenth trigger that day. Every sensory input, every social demand, every small frustration adds water to an invisible bucket. The "cause" wasn't the final drop. It was everything that came before.

Sound support works when other tools fail because it doesn't require anything from your child. No holding. No interaction. No decisions. Just press play. But "calming music" often doesn't work because it has lyrics (adds cognitive load), unpredictable changes (feels threatening), or wasn't designed with sensory sensitivities in mind.

Brown noise, pink noise, and low-frequency sounds tend to work better. Consistent. Predictable. Safe.

The gap between knowing what a meltdown is and knowing what to do feels enormous. But it doesn't have to be.

You deserve better than vague advice. Your child deserves tools that actually work.
