### Slide 3: What a Meltdown Actually Is
Not bad behaviour.
Not manipulation.
Not a tantrum with a fancier name.

A neurological event. Full stop.

When your child hits sensory or emotional overload, their nervous system switches into survival mode. The thinking brain goes offline.

This is why "calm down" doesn't work.

### Slide 4: The Three Phases
PREVENTION
Before the meltdown. Reading warning signs. Building daily regulation.

DURING
The meltdown itself. What to do. What to avoid.

RECOVERY
After the visible crisis ends. The hours, sometimes days, of rebuilding.

Most advice focuses entirely on "during." You need tools for all three.

### Slide 5: The Bucket Truth
The socks didn't cause the meltdown.

They were the tenth trigger that day.

Every sensory input, every social demand, every small frustration adds water to an invisible bucket.

The scratchy school jumper: a splash.
The noisy cafeteria: more water.
The substitute teacher: a big pour.

When it overflows? That's your meltdown.

### Slide 6: During a Meltdown
DO:
• Reduce sensory input
• Stop talking
• Ensure safety
• Stay present but not intrusive
• Wait

DON'T:
• Reason or explain
• Ask questions
• Make demands
• Restrain (unless safety requires it)
• Punish

### Slide 7: What to Actually Play
Brown noise and pink noise
(Deep, consistent sound)

Low-frequency sounds
(Below 500Hz feels grounding)

ASMR sounds
(Gentle tapping, soft brushing)

AVOID:
Music with lyrics
Unpredictable changes
High-pitched sounds
Complete silence

### Slide 8: After-School Meltdowns
Your child holds it together all day at school.

The teacher says they're fine.

Then they walk through your door and explode.

They've been masking all day. By home time, there's nothing left.

The key: Create a transition zone. Low-demand period. No questions. Just space.

### Slide 9: The Meltdown Hangover
The screaming has stopped. But your child isn't okay yet.

Physical exhaustion
Emotional fragility
Cognitive fog
Shame and embarrassment
Memory gaps

Give time before talking. Keep demands low. Be present without pressure.

## Text Post 1

You've read the NHS guidance. You've visited the National Autistic Society website. You've Googled "how to help autism meltdown" at 2am with shaking hands while your child screams in the next room.

And every source says the same thing: "Try calming music."

What music? Which sounds? At what volume?

Nobody answers.

Here's what I wish someone had told me when we were drowning:

1. Meltdowns aren't single events — they have three distinct phases. Prevention (before), During (the crisis), and Recovery (the hours after). You need different tools for each.

2. The socks didn't cause the meltdown — they were the final drop in an already-full bucket. Every sensory input, every social demand, every small frustration throughout the day adds water. The "cause" wasn't the final drop. It was everything that came before.

3. Sound support works when other tools fail — because it doesn't require anything from your child. No holding. No interaction. No decisions. Just press play and let their nervous system receive something consistent and safe.

4. "Calming music" often doesn't work — because it has lyrics (adds cognitive load), unpredictable changes (feels threatening), or wasn't designed with sensory sensitivities in mind. Brown noise, pink noise, and low-frequency sounds tend to work better.

5. Recovery is its own phase — The meltdown appears over, but your child isn't okay yet. Physical exhaustion. Emotional fragility. Cognitive fog. This lasts hours, sometimes days. How you handle recovery matters as much as how you handled the crisis.

The gap between knowing what a meltdown is and knowing what to do feels enormous. But it doesn't have to be.

You deserve better than vague advice. Your child deserves tools that actually work.

## Text Post 2

After-school meltdowns.

Your child holds it together all day at school. The teacher says they're fine. Quiet, even.

Then they walk through your door and explode.

This pattern is so common it has a name. And understanding why it happens changes everything.

Here's what's actually going on:

Your child masks all day at school. They suppress the urge to cover their ears. They force eye contact that feels uncomfortable. They don't move their body the way it needs to move. They absorb sensory input that would overwhelm them if they stopped to feel it.

By home time, there's nothing left.

And when they walk through your door? Into the one place where they don't have to pretend?

Everything floods out at once.

This isn't bad behaviour. It's the masking debt coming due.

Research published in Autism Research (2022) describes how many autistic individuals spend significant mental energy suppressing their natural responses to fit into neurotypical environments.

Understanding this changes how you respond.

The key is creating a transition zone. A low-demand period when they first arrive home where they can decompress without pressure.

No questions about school.
No reminders about homework.
Just space.

And maybe some calming sounds ready to play.

Many parents find that having sound playing when their child walks in the door makes an enormous difference. Not talking. Not asking. Just ambient support while the nervous system discharges six hours of accumulated stress.

Your child isn't being difficult. They're being real with you. That's actually a sign of trust.

How do you support your child's after-school transition?

## Text Post 3

"Just calm down."

Three words that make everything worse during an autism meltdown.

Here's why:

During a meltdown, the thinking part of your child's brain is offline. The survival brain has taken over completely.

"Calm down" requires:
• Processing language
• Understanding instructions
• Consciously regulating

None of which is possible when the thinking brain isn't available.

Telling a child mid-meltdown to calm down is like telling someone drowning to just swim. The capacity isn't there.

So what actually helps during a meltdown?

REDUCE SENSORY INPUT
Dim lights if possible. Lower your voice, or better yet, stop talking entirely. Create physical space. Don't add more stimulation to an already overloaded system.

STOP TALKING
Your child can't process language right now. Saying "it's okay" or "what's wrong?" adds more input to an overwhelmed system. Silence is better.

ENSURE SAFETY
Move dangerous objects. Create a safe space around them. If you're in public, ignore the staring strangers and focus on your child.

WAIT
This is the hardest part. Meltdowns run their course. There's no fast-forward button. No magic words.

Your job is simple but not easy: keep them safe and don't make things worse until their nervous system can reset.

And if you need something that helps without requiring anything from your child? Sound support. Brown noise, pink noise, low-frequency sounds.

Press play. That's it.

The meltdown won't stop instantly. Nothing does. But sound gives the nervous system something consistent to land on while the overwhelm runs its course.
