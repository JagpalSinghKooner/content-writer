### Slide 2: The Problem
During sensory overload, most calming tools ask something of your child.

Weighted blankets need them to tolerate touch.
Fidgets need motor control.
Deep pressure needs them to accept contact.
Breathing exercises need coordination.

Every single one requires something.

When a child is in peak overload, all of these demands become impossible.

### Slide 3: Why Sound is Different
Sound requires nothing.

Your child doesn't need to:
• Reach for it
• Hold it
• Follow instructions
• Coordinate their muscles

The sound simply exists in the space around them.

It enters through their ears without asking permission.

### Slide 4: The Science
Research shows certain sound frequencies signal safety to the nervous system.

Predictable patterns calm → The brain stops scanning for danger
Specific frequencies regulate → Activates "rest and digest" mode
Passive input bypasses resistance → Reaches regulation centres directly

### Slide 5: What Works
ASMR Sounds
Whispered voices, gentle tapping, soft brushing. Provides intimacy without physical contact.

Nature Soundscapes
Rain, ocean waves, forest sounds. Consistent, predictable patterns the nervous system can settle into.

Frequency-Based Sounds
Solfeggio frequencies and binaural beats. Affects brainwave patterns and supports regulation.

### Slide 6: Matching to Your Child
For auditory seekers:
White noise, pink noise, layered soundscapes

For auditory sensitives:
Predictable sounds, controlled volume, simple single-layer sounds

The difference between overwhelming sound and calming sound isn't volume.
It's predictability. Control. The specific qualities of the sound itself.

### Slide 7: When to Use Sound
Not just for crisis moments.

Morning regulation → Start the day settled
Transition support → Bridge between activities
Pre-sleep wind-down → 30-60 minutes before bed
Recovery after overload → Even after distress has passed

Think of it as preventative care for your child's nervous system.

### Slide 8: The Zero-Demand Principle
The calming power of sound comes from its passivity.

Don't ask your child to:
• Choose what to play
• Put on headphones
• Tell you if it's helping
• Do anything except be present with the sound

The more you ask, the more you undermine the benefit.

### Slide 9: What Sound Cannot Do
Sound is powerful. But it's one tool among many.

Sound cannot:
• Replace co-regulation entirely (your calm presence still matters)
• Fix underlying sensory processing differences
• Work immediately the first time (building familiarity takes time)
• Be the only strategy you use

Sound works best as part of a broader approach.

That's all it takes.

## Text Post 2

Most parents think sound-sensitive children can't benefit from calming sounds.

Wrong.

Sound sensitivity isn't about all sounds being bad. It's about unpredictable, uncontrolled sounds feeling threatening.

Here's the difference:

Overwhelming sounds:
• Unpredictable (can't anticipate what's coming)
• Uncontrolled (no volume control, no off switch)
• Complex (multiple layers, competing frequencies)
• Sudden (unexpected starts and stops)

Calming sounds:
• Predictable (consistent patterns the brain can follow)
• Controlled (child can adjust or stop at any time)
• Simple (single-layer, clear frequencies)
• Gradual (gentle starts and stops)

The same child who covers their ears in a noisy classroom can settle beautifully to gentle ASMR or nature sounds.

The key is giving them agency. Let them:
• Choose when it plays
• Control the volume
• Turn it off whenever they want
• Explore different sound types

Therapeutic sound, played at safe volumes with consistent patterns, can actually help sound-sensitive children. The difference is predictability and control.

We've seen this with hundreds of families. Sound-sensitive children who struggle with everyday noise finding comfort in carefully designed soundscapes.

Because it's not about avoiding all sound. It's about providing the right sound at the right time in the right way.

## Text Post 3

Sound isn't just for crisis moments.

That's what I used to think. Put on calming sounds when my child is melting down, hope it helps, move on.

I missed the bigger opportunity.

Building regular sound-based regulation into daily routines can reduce the frequency and intensity of overload episodes. Think of it as preventative care for your child's nervous system.

Here's what that looks like:

Morning regulation:
Start the day with gentle sounds before demands begin. Play soft ambient sounds while they wake. Use calming audio during the getting-ready routine. Offer a few minutes of chosen sounds before leaving for school.

Morning sound helps the nervous system start regulated rather than already climbing toward overload.

Transition support:
Transitions are high-risk moments for sensory overload. Sound can bridge the gap. In the car on the way home from school. When moving between activities. During the shift from active play to quiet time.

Having a "transition sound" that your child associates with settling becomes a powerful cue over time. Their nervous system learns to respond before they even realise it.

Pre-sleep wind-down:
The nervous system needs time to shift from daytime alertness to sleep readiness. For neurodivergent children, this transition often takes longer.

Start calming audio 30-60 minutes before bed. Use the same sounds consistently to build association. Layer sounds with dimmed lighting for combined sensory input.

Recovery after overload:
Even after a meltdown or shutdown passes, the nervous system needs recovery time. Keep sounds playing even after visible distress has passed. Use simpler, less stimulating sounds than during active overload. Don't rush back into demands.

The change isn't immediate. It builds over time. But the families who use sound proactively—not just reactively—report fewer meltdowns, smoother transitions, and better sleep.

Prevention is better than intervention. Sound makes both possible.

How do you use sound with your child? Crisis tool or daily routine?
