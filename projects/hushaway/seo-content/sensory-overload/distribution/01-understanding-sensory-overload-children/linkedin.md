### Slide 2: The Reality
Sensory overload isn't bad behaviour.

It's when the brain receives more input than it can process.

The filtering system that works automatically for other children? It works differently for yours.

### Slide 3: The Cumulative Effect
It's rarely one big trigger.

It's the scratchy clothes in the morning + the echoey hall + the wrong smell in the canteen + the bump in the corridor + the loud afternoon.

By the time they're home, one small thing tips them over.

### Slide 4: The Brain Science
When overload hits, it triggers fight-flight-freeze.

Stress hormones flood the body.
The rational thinking brain goes offline.

This is why you can't reason with them. They literally can't hear you right now.

### Slide 5: Two Types
**Hypersensitive:** The world feels too loud, too bright, too much. Their filtering system lets too much through.

**Hyposensitive:** They don't receive enough sensory input. They seek strong experiences to get what their system needs.

Many children are a mix of both.

## Text Post 2

"Why does my child cover their ears but still like certain sounds?"

This question comes up constantly. And it makes perfect sense once you understand the difference.

Unpredictable sounds trigger the brain's threat response. Sudden noises, overlapping conversations, unexpected alarms. These tell the brain there's potential danger.

Predictable, controlled sounds do the opposite. Steady rhythms, consistent volume, known patterns. These signal safety to the nervous system.

Your child might cover their ears at a fire alarm (unpredictable, alarming) but find deep calm in certain music or soundscapes (predictable, safe).

It's not about sound being good or bad. It's about whether the sound signals safety or threat.

Here's the hard truth about sensory overload: you can't always prevent the bucket from filling during the day.

But you can help empty it when they come home.

Most sensory strategies focus on movement and touch. Weighted blankets. Fidgets. Deep breathing. All valuable.

But they require something from your child. Touch tolerance. Motor control. Cognitive load.

During peak overload? Those things aren't available.

Sound is different. The auditory nerve connects directly to the brainstem and limbic system. It reaches the emotional brain without requiring anything from the child.

No holding. No remembering. No coordinating their body.

Morning sounds help the nervous system wake gently. Transition sounds ease shifts between activities. Background sounds during homework filter out distractions. Evening sounds help wind down.

This is what a sensory diet looks like when it includes the auditory system.

And for exhausted parents? This matters. It's one intervention that doesn't require you to do more.

Press play and get on with making dinner. Press play and sit quietly together. Press play and give yourself a moment to breathe too.
