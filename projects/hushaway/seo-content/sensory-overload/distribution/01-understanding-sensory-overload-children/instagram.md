## Caption

Your child was fine ten minutes ago. Now they're on the floor, hands over their ears, completely unreachable.

.
.
.

You're exhausted. You've tried everything. And you're wondering if you're doing something wrong.

You're not.

This is sensory overload. And it's not what most parents think it is.

It's rarely one big trigger. It's cumulative. The scratchy clothes in the morning, the echoey hall, the wrong smell, the bump in the corridor, the loud afternoon. By the time they walk through your door, their sensory bucket is full to the brim.

One small thing tips them over.

When overload hits, it triggers fight-flight-freeze. The rational thinking brain goes offline. This is why you can't reason with them. They literally can't hear you right now.

But here's what surprised us: the auditory system, the very system that often triggers overload, is also the key to helping them regulate.

Sound reaches the emotional brain faster than any other sense. And the right sound‚Äîpredictable, controlled, designed for sensitive ears‚Äîcan help when other tools can't.

During peak overload, weighted blankets need touch tolerance (often rejected). Fidgets need motor control (unavailable). Deep breathing needs thinking brain (offline).

But sound? They just have to be in the room. Press play. Step back. Let it work.

Your child isn't difficult. Their nervous system processes the world differently. And understanding that changes everything.
