## Caption

Your child covers their ears at fire alarms, hand dryers, birthday parties. You've spent years protecting them from sound.

And now someone tells you sound could actually help them?

.
.
.

Here's what most parents don't understand about auditory hypersensitivity: your child isn't reacting to sound itself. They're reacting to unpredictable, uncontrollable, overwhelming sound.

There's a world of difference between noise that overwhelms and sound that regulates.

Triggering noise has unpredictable timing, variable volume, harsh frequencies, and no end point. Therapeutic sound has a predictable pattern, consistent volume, safe frequencies, and defined duration.

When you understand this distinction, using sound for a sound-sensitive child stops feeling contradictory. You're not adding more noise. You're replacing chaos with pattern.

Watch closely and you'll notice: the same child who screams at hand dryers might hum to themselves constantly. They're already self-regulating with sound. They just need the right sounds made accessible.

After school, when their nervous system has been in survival mode for six hours, silence alone won't bring them down from high alert. Therapeutic sound gives their nervous system a focal point. Something consistent to land on while everything else feels chaotic inside.
