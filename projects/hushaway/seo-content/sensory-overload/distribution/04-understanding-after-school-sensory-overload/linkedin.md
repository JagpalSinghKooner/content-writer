## Text Post 1

Your child is fine at school. Gets home. Explodes.

You've spent all day looking forward to seeing them. And this is what you get.

Here's what's actually happening.

School is a sensory assault course. Even on a "good day," your child has processed six hours of:

• Fluorescent lights flickering
• Twenty-plus children in constant motion
• Social demands (reading faces, managing friendships, performing "normal")
• Physical demands (sitting still, tolerating touch, moving through crowds)

For a neurodivergent or sensitive child, this is exhausting at a cellular level.

There's a name for what you're seeing: restraint collapse. They've been holding it together all day in an environment that demands constant regulation. When they reach you, they've got nothing left.

The meltdown isn't about you doing something wrong. It's proof you've created a space where they feel safe enough to fall apart.

That's not failure. That's trust.

What your child needs first isn't questions, snacks, or hugs. It's sound.

The auditory system processes faster than any other sense. Sound bypasses the demands that make everything else impossible. No decisions. No touch tolerance. No cognitive load. Just press play.

Here's what actually works:

1. Sound on before they're through the door
2. No questions for 20 minutes
3. A designated landing zone with calming sounds playing
4. Snacks within reach, not offered
5. Wait for them to emerge (usually 15-30 minutes)

The conversation you wanted? It happens. Just not in the first twenty minutes.

Your child isn't "saving it for you." They're trusting you with the parts they can't show anyone else.

Your child has just spent six hours performing "normal" in an environment not designed for their nervous system.

School demands:
• Sitting still when their body needs to move

• Reading social cues when they're already overloaded
• Tolerating sensory input (lights, noise, touch) with no escape

When they finally reach you, their system crashes.

This is called restraint collapse. Research shows that the effort of camouflaging autistic traits in social environments leads to significant mental and physical exhaustion.

Your child doesn't melt down AT you. They melt down BECAUSE OF you. Because home is the one place they can finally stop holding it all in.
