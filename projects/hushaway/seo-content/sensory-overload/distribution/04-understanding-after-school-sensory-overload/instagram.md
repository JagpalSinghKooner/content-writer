## Caption

Your child is fine at school. Gets home. Explodes.

.
.
.

Here's what's actually happening.

School is a sensory assault course. Six hours of fluorescent lights, constant noise, reading faces, sitting still, performing "normal." For a neurodivergent or sensitive child, this is exhausting at a cellular level.

There's a name for what you're seeing: restraint collapse. They've been holding it together all day. When they reach you, they've got nothing left.

The meltdown isn't about you doing something wrong. It's proof you've created a space where they feel safe enough to fall apart. That's not failure. That's trust.

What your child needs first isn't questions, snacks, or hugs. It's sound. The auditory system processes faster than any other sense. Sound bypasses the demands that make everything else impossible.

The after-school reset: Sound on before they walk in. No questions for 20 minutes. A landing zone with calming sounds. Snacks within reach, not offered. Wait for the signal.

After 15-30 minutes, most children emerge. Ready to talk. Ready to reconnect. The conversation you wanted? It happens. Just not in the first twenty minutes.

Your child isn't "saving it for you." They're trusting you with the parts they can't show anyone else.
