## Caption

You don't need a clinic to use sound therapy with your child.

After 7 years running a nursery and training as a sound therapy practitioner, here's what I've learned: the biggest barrier isn't access to practitioners. It's the belief that you need them at all.

.
.
.

If you've ever put on nature sounds at bedtime and watched your child's breathing slow, you've already done a form of sound therapy. The only difference between that and "proper" sound therapy is intention and consistency.

Here's what actually works:

BEDTIME ‚Üí Nature sounds, gentle instrumental, ASMR. Start during bedtime routine (not when they're already in bed). Keep volume low. Same sounds nightly.

FOCUS ‚Üí Binaural beats, white/pink/brown noise. From the start of homework, not when focus is lost. Creates environment for concentration.

OVERWHELM ‚Üí Frequencies (174 Hz, 285 Hz), simple ambient sounds. For recovery and prevention, not during acute meltdown.

TRANSITIONS ‚Üí Predictable sequences. Same sounds each morning, after school, before meals. Routine builds association.

The biggest mistake? Only reaching for sound during crisis. Sound therapy builds over time. Your child's brain learns: "This sound means calm is coming." That requires repetition.

You already have the equipment. Phone, speaker, quiet-ish space. That's it.

Start with one routine. One situation that's hard in your household right now. Add sound to that context for two weeks straight. Same sound, same situation, every day.

Sometimes one quiet moment changes a whole day. That's worth having.
