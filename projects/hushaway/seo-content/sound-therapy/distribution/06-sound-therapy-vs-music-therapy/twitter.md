### Tweet 2
1. Music therapy = ACTIVE engagement

Sessions with a therapist. Instruments. Participation. Structure.

Your child responds, creates, interacts.

It's brilliant. But it requires capacity.

### Tweet 3
2. Sound therapy = PASSIVE listening

Press play. Your child receives the sounds. No demands. No responses expected.

It works whether or not they're consciously listening.

### Tweet 4
3. Why this matters for ND kids:

Think about your child at their most overwhelmed. Mid-meltdown. End of school day.

Can they follow instructions in that moment? Participate in anything?

Most can't. The tank is empty.

### Tweet 5
4. Music therapy needs something left in the tank

Your child needs enough regulation to:
- Participate in the session
- Follow the therapist's guidance
- Respond to musical cues

On a hard day, that capacity just isn't there.

### Tweet 6
5. Sound therapy asks nothing

Your child can:
- Lie in bed
- Sit in a car seat
- Curl up on the sofa
- Hide under a blanket

The sounds work anyway. No performance required.

### Tweet 7
6. The evidence picture

Music therapy: stronger research base, decades of studies

Sound therapy: newer, more mixed evidence

But "more evidence" â‰  "better for YOUR child"

Choose based on capacity, not research volume.

### Tweet 8
7. You can use BOTH

Music therapy weekly for structured work when your child has capacity.

Sound therapy daily for regulation, crisis moments, and the gaps between sessions.

They're complementary, not competing.

### Tweet 9
8. The real question

What does your child need RIGHT NOW?

Not where you hope they'll be in 6 months. Today.

If they can't engage, start with what asks nothing of them.
