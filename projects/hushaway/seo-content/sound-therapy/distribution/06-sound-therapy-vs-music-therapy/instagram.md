## Caption

Your child tried music therapy. It didn't work. Now you're wondering if sound-based approaches just aren't for your family.

.
.
.

Here's what nobody told you: they're completely different approaches.

Music therapy is active. Sessions with a therapist. Instruments. Participation. It's brilliant and evidence-based. But it requires capacity. Your child needs something left in the tank to engage.

Sound therapy is passive. Press play. Your child receives the sounds. No demands. No performance. No responses expected. It works whether or not they're consciously listening.

Think about your child at their most overwhelmed. Mid-meltdown. End of the school day when everything feels like too much. Bedtime when their racing brain won't switch off.

Can they follow instructions in those moments? Participate in anything at all?

For most neurodivergent children, the answer is no. When the tank is empty, there's nothing left to give. That's when passive approaches work. They ask nothing of your child. Nothing of you.

If music therapy didn't quite fit, it doesn't mean you failed. It doesn't mean your child failed. It might just mean they needed something different. Something that met them in their struggle instead of asking them to rise above it.
