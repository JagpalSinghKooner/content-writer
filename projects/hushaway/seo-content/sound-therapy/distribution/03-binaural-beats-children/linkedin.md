### Slide 2: What They Are
Binaural beats aren't sounds.

They're an auditory illusion your brain creates when different frequencies reach each ear.

200 Hz left ear + 210 Hz right ear = 10 Hz perceived beat.

### Slide 5: When to Avoid
Extra caution needed if your child has:

• Significant auditory sensitivities

## Text Post 1

You typed "are binaural beats safe for children" into Google at 11pm.

We understand that search.

Here's the honest answer most websites won't give you: the research on binaural beats for children is limited. We don't have large studies proving they work for kids. We also don't have evidence they're harmful.

What we can say confidently:

1. **Binaural beats produce a real phenomenon.** Your brain genuinely creates that perceived beat when different frequencies reach each ear. The frequency-following response appears to be a genuine brain mechanism.

2. **The evidence is mixed.** Studies show modest improvements in attention and anxiety reduction in some cases. Others show no effect. And most research has been with adults, not children.

3. **The risks appear low.** Unlike medication, there's no chemical entering your child's body. The main risks are indirect: hearing damage from excessive volume, frustration if expectations aren't met, time spent on something that might not help.

4. **Individual responses vary enormously.** Some children find binaural beats helpful for settling into homework mode. Others find wearing headphones restrictive or the sounds annoying.

5. **Requirements matter.** Stereo headphones are required. Not optional. Binaural beats only work when different frequencies reach each ear separately. Playing through speakers eliminates the binaural effect entirely.

Should you try binaural beats with your child?

That depends on your circumstances, your child's preferences, and your expectations. If you're looking for a guaranteed solution, you'll be disappointed. Nothing is guaranteed for every child.

If you're looking for one low-risk thing to try as part of a broader approach? It might be worth exploring.

One quiet moment can change a whole day for a child. Whether that moment comes from binaural beats, nature sounds, or simply sitting together in silence, the goal is the same: helping your child feel safe, settled, and understood.

## Text Post 2

Parents often ask me: "Which binaural beat frequency should I use for my child?"

Here's the practical guide based on what you're trying to support:

**For deep sleep:**
Delta (1-4 Hz) when your child is already sleepy and settling for bedtime.

**For relaxation:**
Theta (4-8 Hz) during wind-down time, after sensory overload, or when anxiety is high.

**For calm focus:**
Alpha (8-13 Hz) before homework, during transitions, or when you need them to settle into an activity.

**For active attention:**
Beta (13-20 Hz) before focused tasks that require concentration.

But here's what matters more than exact frequencies:

1. **Consistency.** Same time each day for at least two weeks before judging if it helps.

2. **Volume.** Background level, not prominent. If you can hear it across the room while they're wearing headphones, it's too loud.

3. **Your child's response.** If they dislike it, stop. There are many sound approaches, and binaural beats are just one option.

4. **Realistic expectations.** Binaural beats aren't a switch you flip. If they help at all, the effect builds gradually with consistent use.

Don't obsess over exact frequencies. A 6 Hz theta beat and a 7 Hz theta beat aren't meaningfully different for practical purposes.
