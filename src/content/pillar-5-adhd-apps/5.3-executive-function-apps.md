## What Is Executive Function and Why Does It Matter?

Executive function is the brain's command centre. It handles planning, working memory, impulse control, and task initiation. For children with ADHD, these skills develop differently. It's neurology. Not laziness.

Research from 2024 found that [89% of children with ADHD show impairments](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11485171/) in at least one executive function area, with 75-85% struggling with working memory. That's a real difference in how their brains work.

Here is what that looks like in daily life:

- Starting homework feels impossible, even when they know exactly what to do
- They forget instructions between the kitchen and their bedroom
- Time blindness makes five minutes feel identical to fifty
- Organising a school bag becomes overwhelming

Most executive function ADHD apps try to compensate for these challenges with reminders, visual schedules, and reward systems. On paper, this makes sense. In practice, there's a problem. A big one.

## The Catch-22 of Executive Function ADHD Apps

Most parents try this last.

It should come first.

Executive function apps require executive function to use them. Your child needs to remember to open the app, focus long enough to engage with it, plan how to use the features, and maintain the motivation to keep using it over time.

But those are the skills they don't have.

The families I work with describe this pattern: initial excitement, a burst of engagement, then the app joins the graveyard of abandoned solutions. We've seen this countless times. It's heartbreaking.

[CHADD](https://chadd.org/adhd-news/adhd-news-caregivers/brain-training-options-for-adhd-new-fact-sheet/), the national ADHD resource, acknowledges this reality: brain training apps improve performance on specific tasks they train, but there's limited evidence these skills transfer to real life. ## Which Executive Function Apps Help Some Families

Every child is different. What works for one family gets abandoned within days by another. It depends. Here are the executive function apps for children that UK parents mention most often.

### Visual Planners

[Tiimo](https://www.tiimoapp.com/) was named iPhone App of the Year in 2025 for its neurodivergent-first design. It uses visual schedules with colour-coded time blocks, reducing the cognitive load of reading long task lists. Some parents find the visual approach clicks where text-based apps failed.

**Thruday** offers similar visual planning built for ADHD brains, with a focus on making time visible rather than abstract.

### Working Memory Apps

**Cogmed** and **Jungle Memory** target working memory training directly. [Research from 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40750997/) shows neurofeedback and brain training produce improvements in working memory and self-control. But improvements in the app don't always translate to homework time.

### Routine Builders

**Brili** turns morning and bedtime routines into visual sequences, breaking overwhelming tasks into single steps. **Goally** takes this further with dedicated hardware, though the cost puts it out of reach for many UK families.

### What They All Have in Common

Every one of these apps requires engagement. Your child needs to tap, swipe, and interact. They need to remember to check the app and maintain focus while using it. That's a lot to ask.

For children whose nervous systems are overwhelmed, adding another thing that demands active participation backfires. And that brings us to something most parents overlook. This is why many families also look at [LINK TO: Task Management Apps Kids] for different approaches.

## Medication Helps Attention but Doesn't Teach Skills

"My child is on ADHD medication. Why is he still struggling with organisation and planning?"

Parents ask this all the time.

The answer matters.

ADHD medication improves attention and reduces impulsivity. It creates a window where learning becomes possible. But it doesn't teach the skills themselves.

Think of glasses for poor vision. The glasses help you see, but they don't teach you to read. You still need to learn that skill.

Executive function skills need explicit teaching and supported practice. Apps are part of this, alongside occupational therapy, school support, and strategies you use at home.

But if your child's nervous system is overwhelmed before the learning even starts, the window medication creates stays closed.

## How to Choose What Works for Your Child

You know your child best. Here's a framework that helps some families:

**Start with regulation, not task management.**
Before introducing any ADHD organisation apps for children, ask whether your child's nervous system is calm enough to engage. If not, sound support like HushAway® costs nothing to try and requires nothing from your child except being in the same room.

**Match the app to the specific struggle.**
Working memory difficulties need different support than task initiation problems. An app targeting routines won't help a child who can't remember what they just read.

**Accept that novelty fades.**
ADHD brains habituate to rewards faster than neurotypical brains. The app that works in week one gets forgotten by week three. Build in variety rather than relying on a single solution.

**Remove the engagement barrier.**
The fewer steps between your child and the support, the more likely they'll use it. HushAway® requires one tap. Passive support that asks nothing beats active engagement that never happens.

## When to Seek Professional Support

Sometimes the support your child needs goes beyond what apps or sound provide. That's okay. Really.

If your child's executive function challenges are affecting school performance, family relationships, or their own wellbeing, consider:

- **Occupational therapy** for personalised strategies that work with your child's specific profile
- **Educational psychology assessment** to understand where the difficulties lie
- **CAMHS referral** through your GP if mental health support is needed

[ADHD UK](https://adhduk.co.uk/) offers resources and support for families working through the system. The waiting lists are long, but getting on them early matters.

Professional support doesn't mean you've failed. It means you're taking your child's needs seriously. That matters. For children who feel everything, professional guidance makes a real difference alongside tools like the [LINK TO: Sound Sanctuary].
