# HushAway® Content Humanisation Rules

**Zero Tolerance Policy:** Any violation of these rules = automatic fail. Content must be rewritten before publishing.

This file enforces human-sounding content and prevents AI detection. All rules are strict and non-negotiable.

## 3. Banned Phrases (Zero Allowed)

These phrases are dead giveaways for AI-generated content.

### Self-Answering Questions

**BANNED:** Question followed immediately by answer in same paragraph.

**Bad:** "What is the best app for ADHD? The answer is: it depends on your child."
**Why it's AI:** Artificial structure that no human uses in natural writing.
**Good:** "Parents often ask me which app is best. I wish I had a simple answer, but every child is different."

**Bad:** "Do ADHD apps actually work? The honest answer is that some do, some don't."
**Good:** "Whether ADHD apps work depends entirely on the app and the child using it."

### Labelled Transitions

**BANNED:** Transitions that announce what's coming with a colon.

| Banned Phrase | Use Instead |
|---------------|-------------|
| "The honest answer:" | Just give the answer |
| "The takeaway?" / "The takeaway:" | Integrate into closing naturally |
| "What this means:" | "So..." or integrate meaning into previous sentence |
| "The pattern we see:" | Just describe the pattern |
| "Key findings:" | "The studies found..." |
| "Important caveats:" | "But..." or "One thing to know..." |
| "Here's the thing:" | Just say the thing |
| "Here is something most guides miss:" | Just say what they miss |
| "The bottom line:" | Just give the bottom line |
| "The reality is:" | Just state the reality |
| "The truth is:" | Just state the truth |
| "In other words:" | Restructure to be clear the first time |
| "Simply put:" | Write simply the first time |
| "To put it simply:" | Write simply the first time |
| "Think of it this way:" | Just use the metaphor |
| "Consider this:" | Just present it |

**Bad:** "The pattern we see: Parents download an app, their child uses it for three days, then it gets forgotten."
**Good:** "I hear this story constantly. Parents download an app, their child loves it for three days, then it collects dust."

**Bad:** "What this means for choosing apps: You should start with regulation support."
**Good:** "So if your child struggles with big feelings, start with regulation support rather than productivity apps."

## 4. Hedging Density Rules

Hedging (may, might, can, could) is necessary for accuracy but becomes an AI flag when overused.

### Hard Limit

**Maximum 8 hedges per 1000 words.**

For a 3,000-word hub article: maximum 24 hedges total.
For a 1,500-word cluster article: maximum 12 hedges total.

### Words That Count as Hedges

- may
- might
- can (when meaning "is able to" not "is permitted to")
- could
- potentially
- possibly
- perhaps
- likely
- unlikely
- tend to / tends to
- often (when used as hedge, not frequency)

### Stacked Hedges (Never Allowed)

**BANNED:** Multiple hedges in same sentence.

**Bad:** "This may potentially help your child could possibly feel calmer."
**Good:** "This helps many children feel calmer."

**Bad:** "Research suggests this might possibly be effective."
**Good:** "Research suggests this works."

### When Hedging Is Appropriate

- Citing research that isn't conclusive
- Describing outcomes that vary by child
- Making claims about future results

### When to Remove Hedges

- Stating facts
- Describing your own product/approach
- Quoting research with strong conclusions

**Bad:** "HushAway® may help your child feel calmer."
**Good:** "HushAway® helps children feel calmer."

**Bad:** "Sound therapy can potentially support regulation."
**Good:** "Sound therapy supports regulation."

## 5. Sentence Variety Rules

Monotonous sentence structure flags AI content.

### Paragraph Starters

**RULE:** No section can have 3+ paragraphs starting with the same word.

**Bad:**
> This approach works for many families.
>
> This means starting with regulation support.
>
> This is why we built HushAway® differently.
>
> This can make a real difference.

**Good:**
> Many families find this approach works.
>
> Starting with regulation support makes sense here.
>
> We built HushAway® with this principle in mind.
>
> The difference can be significant.

### "This" Sentence Limit

**RULE:** Maximum 2 sentences starting with "This" per section.

Alternatives to starting with "This":
- Start with the subject
- Start with a time/place reference
- Start with an action
- Start with a quote
- Use a conjunction (And, But, So)

### Short Sentence Requirement

**RULE:** Every major section (H2) must include at least one sentence under 8 words.

**Examples of effective short sentences:**
- "It depends."
- "Every child is different."
- "Sound works passively."
- "That changes everything."
- "Many parents feel this."
- "Structure helps."
- "But here's the problem."

### List Structure Variety

**RULE:** Lists must vary grammatical structure. Not every item can follow the same pattern.

**Bad (identical structure):**
- **White noise**: Creates consistent ambient sound for focus
- **Brown noise**: Creates deeper sounds for calm
- **Binaural beats**: Creates frequency patterns for relaxation

**Good (varied structure):**
- **White noise** creates consistent ambient sound that helps filter distractions
- **Brown noise** is deeper, often described as a "weighted blanket for the brain"
- **Binaural beats**: when different tones play in each ear, the brain perceives a third tone

### Additional List Rules

- Do not start every bullet with a verb
- Do not start every bullet with "The"
- Vary bullet lengths (some short, some longer)
- Do not always use exactly 5 bullets (use 4, 6, or 7 sometimes)
- Avoid parallel sentence patterns within every list

### Sentence-Initial Adverb Limit

**RULE:** Maximum 2 sentences starting with "-ly" adverbs per article.

**Adverbs to limit at start of sentences:**
- "Interestingly,..."
- "Unfortunately,..."
- "Importantly,..."
- "Surprisingly,..."
- "Notably,..."
- "Significantly,..."
- "Remarkably,..."
- "Understandably,..."

**Why:** AI overuses sentence-initial adverbs. Vary your sentence starters instead.

### Pronoun Balance Requirements

**RULE:** Avoid AI pronoun patterns.

**Requirements:**
- Maximum 3 consecutive sentences starting with "you" or "your"
- Include "we" language at least twice per article (for community feel)
- Do not start more than 2 paragraphs in a row with "It"

**Why:** AI overuses "you/your" in every sentence and rarely uses inclusive "we" language.

**Good:** "We've seen this pattern countless times in our community. Parents try app after app, hoping the next one will be different."

**Bad:** "You might find this frustrating. Your child might struggle. You could try..."

### "And/But" Sentence Starters (Required)

**RULE:** Minimum 2 sentences starting with "And" or "But" per article.

**Why:** These signal human, conversational writing. AI avoids starting sentences with conjunctions.

**Good examples:**
- "And that is exactly the problem."
- "But here is what most guides miss."
- "And honestly? It works."

### Section Length Variety

**RULE:** Sections should vary in length.

- Not every section should be same length
- Avoid always having exactly 3 main points
- Some sections can be shorter for emphasis
- One section being notably longer is fine

**Why:** AI creates artificial symmetry with perfectly balanced sections.

### Backward Reference Limit

**RULE:** Maximum 1 use of "as mentioned earlier/above/previously" per article.

**Banned phrases (after first use):**
- "as mentioned earlier"
- "as stated above"
- "as previously discussed"
- "as noted earlier"

**Why:** AI overuses backward references. Just restate the point or trust readers remember.

## 6. Human Voice Requirements

Content must include markers of human writing.

### Contractions (Required)

**RULE:** Minimum 2 contractions per 500 words.

Use:
- you're, you'll, you've
- it's, that's, there's
- don't, doesn't, didn't, can't, won't
- I'm, I've, I'll
- we're, we've, we'll
- they're, they've

**Bad:** "You will find that it is not always easy. There is no single answer."
**Good:** "You'll find it's not always easy. There's no single answer."

### Rhetorical Questions (Required)

**RULE:** At least 1 rhetorical question per article that isn't immediately answered.

**Bad (immediately answered):**
"Which app is best? The answer is that it depends on your child."

**Good (creates curiosity, answered later):**
"Which ones actually work? Which are worth paying for? And why do so many seem to lose their appeal after the first week?"

[Answer comes paragraphs later, not immediately]

### Concrete Specificity (Required)

**RULE:** Minimum 3 concrete, specific examples per article (not generic).

**Generic (bad):**
"Many parents struggle with bedtime routines."

**Specific (good):**
"One mum told me she'd tried four different routine apps. Her son loved each one for about three days, then refused to look at them."

**Generic (bad):**
"Apps can help with various challenges."

**Specific (good):**
"The morning routine chart was useless until we started playing brown noise while he got ready. Now he actually follows it."

### Incomplete Thoughts / Interruptions

Use occasionally for natural rhythm:

- "The problem is... well, most apps assume your child can already engage."
- "Focus apps, productivity apps, routine apps, task managers... the list goes on."
- "And that's exactly the issue."

## 7a. Structural Patterns to Avoid

### Perfect Parallel Structure in Every List

**RULE:** Avoid making every bullet point grammatically identical.

**Too perfect (AI-like):**
- Provides consistent ambient sound
- Reduces environmental distractions
- Creates predictable sensory input
- Supports nervous system regulation

**More natural:**
- Provides consistent ambient sound
- Helps filter out distracting noises
- The predictability matters for sensory-sensitive children
- Many parents report it helps with regulation

### Overly Balanced Arguments

**RULE:** Avoid perfect "on one hand... on the other hand" balance.

**Too balanced (AI-like):**
"On one hand, ADHD apps can provide valuable support. On the other hand, they have limitations. While some children benefit greatly, others find them unhelpful."

**More natural:**
"Some ADHD apps genuinely help. Others are a waste of money. The tricky part is figuring out which is which for your child."

### Formulaic Section Endings

**RULE:** Don't end every section the same way (e.g., always linking to HushAway®).

Vary your section endings:
- Some end with a parent quote
- Some end with a question
- Some end with a statement
- Some end with a HushAway® mention (but not all)

## 8. Community Voice Sourcing Policy

### Quote Categories

**Category 1: Verified Quotes (Preferred)**
- Actual quotes from HushAway® community members
- Quotes from testimonials on hushaway.com
- Must be used with permission or anonymised
- Attribution: "One HushAway® mum shared..." or "A parent in our community told us..."

**Category 2: Representative Composites (Acceptable)**
- Synthesised from multiple real parent experiences
- Based on patterns observed in parent forums, support groups, research
- Must reflect genuine, documented parent sentiments
- Attribution: "Parents often tell us..." or "One mum put it perfectly..."
- NOT: "A specific parent said..." (implies direct quote)

**Category 3: Illustrative Examples (Use Sparingly)**
- Created to illustrate a common experience
- Must be clearly plausible and grounded in reality
- Should match language real parents use
- Attribution: "Many parents describe..." or weave into narrative
- MAX: 1 per article (prefer Categories 1-2)

### Quote Authenticity Checklist

Before including any community quote:
- Does this reflect real parent language (not AI/clinical)?
- Would a real parent recognise this as their experience?
- Is the attribution appropriate for the quote source?
- Does it add genuine emotional resonance (not filler)?
- Is it specific enough to feel real?

### Quote Language Markers (Human Signals)

**Real parent quotes include:**
- Contractions ("I'm," "we've," "doesn't")
- Incomplete thoughts ("It's just... you know")
- Specific details (brand names, ages, times)
- Emotional language ("exhausted," "guilt," "relief")
- UK colloquialisms ("knackered," "at my wit's end")

**Avoid AI-sounding quotes:**
- Perfect grammar and complete sentences
- Generic language without specific details
- Clinical or formal tone
- Phrases no parent would actually say

### Examples

**Good (feels real):**
"We've tried every app going. He loves them for three days, then they collect dust. I realised we were trying to build the roof before the foundation."

**Bad (feels AI-generated):**
"We have found that various applications provide temporary engagement but lack long-term effectiveness for our child's needs."

**Good (specific, emotional):**
"The 2am bedtime battles were killing me. I was sitting outside his door, crying, wondering what I was doing wrong."

**Bad (generic, flat):**
"Bedtime can be challenging for parents of neurodivergent children."

## 8b. External Link Requirements

External links build E-E-A-T credibility. Every article needs authoritative external sources.

### Minimum External Links

| Article Type | Minimum External Links |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Hub articles | 3 |
| Cluster articles | 2 |

### UK-Approved Sources (Preferred)

These sources are automatically approved:
- NHS (nhs.uk)
- ADHD UK (adhduk.co.uk)
- NICE guidelines (nice.org.uk)
- British Psychological Society (bps.org.uk)
- Royal College of Psychiatrists (rcpsych.ac.uk)
- UK Government (gov.uk)
- PubMed (ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed)

### Sources Requiring Review

Non-UK sources (e.g., .com, CDC, NIMH) will be flagged as warnings. These may be acceptable if:
- No UK equivalent exists for the research
- The source is a peer-reviewed journal
- The information is foundational/widely cited

### What NOT to Link

- Other blogs (unless highly authoritative)
- Social media
- Wikipedia (use original sources)
- Commercial sites with bias
- Outdated government pages

## 9. Curiosity Loop Templates

Use these to create mid-article engagement. Minimum 2 per hub article, 1 per cluster article.

### By Section Type

**After describing a problem:**
- "But here is the question most guides never ask: [question]?"
- "So what actually helps? The answer might surprise you."
- "There is one thing these approaches have in common."

**Before revealing a solution:**
- "Which brings us to something most parents overlook."
- "And that is where things get interesting."
- "But there is another way."

**After listing options:**
- "So which type does your child actually need?"
- "The difference matters more than you might think."
- "One of these works very differently from the rest."

**Transitioning between competitor sections:**
- "But what if your child cannot engage with any app right now?"
- "That works well when children are calm. But what about the moments they are not?"
- "These apps assume something that is not always true."

**Building anticipation:**
- "There is a reason this approach works when others fail."
- "Most parents try this last. It should probably come first."
- "This is the bit most articles leave out."

### What NOT to Use

- "You won't believe what happens next!" (clickbait)
- Questions you do not answer in the following section
- More than 3 curiosity loops per article (overuse)
